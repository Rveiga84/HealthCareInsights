# Exploratory Analysis of Insurance Data

Introduction

I wanted to learn more about our insurance customers. So, I looked at three things: how old they are, their body health score (called BMI), and how much they pay for insurance.

Age Distribution

Histogram Analysis:

The age distribution is fairly uniform, with the majority of customers falling between ages 20 to 60. This indicates that the dataset encompasses a broad age range, from young adults to older individuals.

Key Observations:

A significant number of customers are in their early twenties, suggesting that many people opt for insurance at a young age, possibly due to employment benefits or early financial planning. There’s a steady presence of customers across all age groups, ensuring that our further analysis can be generalized across different age demographics.

Body Mass Index (BMI) Distribution

Histogram Analysis:

The BMI histogram showcases that a majority of the insured individuals have a BMI ranging from 25 to 35. This range typically classifies individuals as “Obese” or “Overweight”, indicating a potential health concern for a significant portion of our dataset.

Key Observations:

A spike in the frequency around the 30 mark suggests that many individuals are on the threshold between “Obese” and “Overweight”, indicating potential health risks. Fewer individuals lie in the “Underweight” and “Normal” BMI categories, pointing towards an increasing trend in overweight issues among the insured.

Insurance Charges Distribution

Histogram Analysis:

The distribution of insurance charges is right-skewed, indicating that a majority of the insured individuals have lower charges. However, there’s a noticeable tail, suggesting that a minority of individuals have significantly higher charges.

Key Observations:

The bulk of customers pay a relatively low insurance charge, which might correspond to basic insurance plans or group benefits. The tail end with higher charges could represent individuals with pre-existing health conditions, higher risk profiles, or premium insurance plans.

Insurance Charges Analysis by Age, BMI, and Smoking Status

Objective:

To understand the intricate relationships between age, BMI, smoking habits, and insurance charges. This deep-dive aims to identify specific trends and correlations that could inform both consumers and insurance providers about the determinants of insurance charges.

Methods:

The dataset was segmented into five age groups. Within each age group, charges were further divided based on four BMI categories: Underweight, Normal, Overweight, and Obese. Each of these segments has two distinct data points: charges for smokers and charges for non-smokers. The results were visualized in a grouped bar chart.

Bar chart with average charges by Age and BMI Groups, separated by Non-Smokers and Smokers

Findings:

Age Trend: Across all BMI categories, older age groups tend to have higher insurance charges. This is consistent with the understanding that aging is accompanied by increased health risks, making older individuals more costly to insure.

The Dominance of Smoking: Regardless of age or BMI, smokers invariably face steeper insurance charges than their non-smoking counterparts. This gap in charges is particularly pronounced among individuals with higher BMIs, signaling the multiplicative health risks posed by smoking and being overweight.

BMI’s Significant Influence: Within each age bracket, transitioning from a “Normal” BMI to an “Obese” BMI sees a marked hike in insurance charges for both smokers and non-smokers. While all elevated BMI levels influence charges, the “Obese” category stands out distinctly. Among all the groups analyzed, individuals classified as “Obese” consistently bear the heaviest insurance charges. This holds true irrespective of age or smoking habits, underscoring the substantial financial implications of obesity in the realm of health insurance.

Conclusion:

Upon analyzing the dataset related to insurance charges, I identified a distinct pattern linking Body Mass Index (BMI) to insurance costs. Specifically, individuals categorized as ‘obese’ by BMI standards tend to incur higher insurance charges than those in other BMI classifications.

Recommendations:

Health Initiatives: Introducing health initiatives that focus on individuals in the ‘obese’ BMI bracket can be beneficial. This approach can enhance health outcomes for policyholders and potentially reduce claims in the long run.

Insurance Premium Structuring: It might be constructive to weigh BMI more heavily when determining insurance premiums, ensuring that rates are commensurate with health indicators.

Educational Campaigns: By emphasizing the significance of a balanced BMI and its impact on insurance costs, we can guide individuals towards healthier living choices.

Engagement with Health Experts: Forming alliances with health professionals to facilitate regular health assessments or provide fitness programs at concessional rates can be considered. This strategy can be particularly effective for those in the ‘obese’ BMI range, emphasizing preventive care.

Incentive-Based Pricing: A pricing structure that offers financial incentives or reduced premiums for those who maintain or achieve a balanced BMI could encourage a healthier lifestyle among policyholders.

Predictive Modeling

Why Use a Predictive Model?

Imagine you have a magic crystal ball. When you ask it a question about the future, it gives you an answer. That’s kind of what a predictive model does! A predictive model looks at information we already know and uses it to make smart guesses about things we want to find out. In this project, I’m using a predictive model to guess how much money someone might pay for insurance in the future. This helps insurance companies plan better, and it can also help people understand what to expect when they buy insurance.
